A Voice of Revolution and Brotherhood

Jaswant Singh Rahi was born on 16 March 1913 in the sacred town of Dera Baba Nanak, Gurdaspur district, Punjab — and he never left. His roots ran as deep as the rivers of his homeland, and it was from this soil that one of Punjabi literature's most honest and enduring voices emerged.

Born into a Rajput (Jaswal) family devoted to India's independence movement, Rahi was drawn from youth toward the twin fires of patriotism and literature. Inspired by the freedom struggle, he joined the Communist movement and formally adopted the name Rahi — meaning "the traveller on the path" — as a symbol of his ideological journey.

He was a close companion of Baba Pyare Lal Bedi, the Punjabi Sikh philosopher, and became a mentor to some of the greatest names in Punjabi letters. The legendary poet Shiv Kumar Batalvi spent weeks at Rahi's home in Dera Baba Nanak during his formative years, absorbing the elder poet's wisdom.

Rahi's life was one of fearless authenticity. Critics noted that his three-part autobiography, Main Kiven Jeeveya (How I Lived), concealed nothing — every incident recorded with unflinching honesty, regardless of consequence.

Literary Legacy

The Progressive Bard

Rahi's literary vision was rooted in the progressive movement that transformed 20th-century Punjabi letters. His poetry does not merely protest — it demands transformation: of the self, of society, of the relationship between human beings.

Scholar Manpreet Kaur wrote that Rahi "begins his poems with the repugnancy existing between imagination and realism," seeking to liberate individuals from "communalism, slavery and inhuman behaviour."

"Rahi's contribution to the Punjabi world of letters is no less than that of Dhani Ram Chatrik, Prof Mohan Singh, and Prof Puran Singh."
— Joginder Singh Bedi, Columnist, The Tribune
"Rahi has his own unique style of writing which is not influenced by any other prominent or popular author."
— Kulbir Singh Kang, Literary Critic
